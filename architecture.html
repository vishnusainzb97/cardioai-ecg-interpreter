Behind the Technology

How does a computer learn to read a heartbeat? No jargon. No PhD required. Just clear explanations of the AI that powers CardioAI.

Starting Simple

What is Deep Learning?

Deep Learning is a way for computers to learn patterns from data — much like how a child learns to recognize things by seeing thousands of examples. Instead of being told every rule, the computer figures out the rules on its own.

Traditional Programming

A human writes every rule by hand. For example: "If the heart rate is above 100 AND the rhythm is irregular, flag it."

This works for simple cases, but ECGs have thousands of subtle patterns that are nearly impossible to hand-code for every scenario.

Deep Learning Approach

We show the computer thousands of ECGs along with what a doctor diagnosed. Over time, the computer learns to spot the same patterns a cardiologist would — all by itself.

Think of it like teaching by example, not by instruction. You show a child 1,000 pictures of cats and dogs — soon they can tell the difference without being told the rules.

The Learning Process

How Does AI Learn to Read an ECG?

The training process is surprisingly intuitive. Here's how the model goes from knowing nothing to interpreting complex heart rhythms.

1

Gather Thousands of Real ECGs

We start with over 21,000 real clinical ECGs from trusted open-source databases like PTB-XL (PhysioNet). Each one has been diagnosed by at least two certified cardiologists.

2

Show Each ECG to the Model

The model sees an ECG and makes a guess — "I think this is Atrial Fibrillation." At first, it's usually wrong. Like a medical student on day one, it hasn't learned the patterns yet.

3

Check Against the Doctor's Answer

We compare the model's guess to the real diagnosis. If wrong, we calculate how wrong and in what direction. This "error signal" tells the model exactly what to adjust.

4

Adjust and Repeat — Millions of Times

The model tweaks its internal parameters to reduce its error. This cycle — guess, check, adjust — repeats millions of times. Gradually, the model develops an intuition for ECG patterns.

5

Test on ECGs It Has Never Seen

We test the trained model on a completely separate set of ECGs. This proves it actually learned patterns. Our model achieves a 94.3% F1 Score on this unseen test set.

The Core Model

What is a CNN?

CNN stands for Convolutional Neural Network. Don't worry about the name — a CNN is specifically designed to spot patterns in sequences (like heartbeat signals) by scanning small windows across the data, piece by piece.

The Magnifying Glass Analogy

Imagine sliding a small magnifying glass across an ECG printout. At each position, the glass highlights a tiny section. The CNN does the same — it slides a "filter" across the signal, looking for spikes, dips, or irregular gaps.

By stacking many filters together, the CNN can detect complex combinations of patterns — just like a doctor scans for QRS complexes, P-waves, and ST segments all at once.

Layers of Understanding

A CNN has multiple layers, each learning something different:

  • Layer 1: Basic shapes — sharp peaks, flat lines, small wiggles
  • Layer 2: Combinations — a peak followed by a dip (like a QRS complex)
  • Layer 3: Full rhythm patterns — Atrial Fibrillation vs. Normal Sinus

How Our CNN Processes Your ECG

1

Scan the Signal

The raw ECG waveform enters as a sequence of numbers representing voltage over time.

2

Detect Features

Convolutional filters slide across, each hunting for a different pattern — peaks, dips, irregularities.

3

Compress & Focus

Pooling layers shrink data, keeping only the most important features — the key moments in the heartbeat.

4

Make a Decision

The final layer weighs all evidence and outputs a probability for each condition. The highest wins.

Model Details

1D CNN Architecture

A residual 1D convolutional network optimized for ECG signal classification, extracting temporal features at multiple scales.

Layer Type Configuration Output Shape
Input Input 12-lead ECG signal (5000, 12)
Conv Block 1 Conv1D 64 filters, kernel=7, ReLU (5000, 64)
BatchNorm 1 BatchNorm Normalize + ReLU (5000, 64)
MaxPool 1 MaxPool Pool=2, stride=2 (2500, 64)
Conv Block 2 Conv1D 128 filters, kernel=5, ReLU (2500, 128)
BatchNorm 2 BatchNorm Normalize + ReLU (2500, 128)
MaxPool 2 MaxPool Pool=2, stride=2 (1250, 128)
Conv Block 3 Conv1D 256 filters, kernel=3, ReLU (1250, 256)
Global AvgPool GAP Global average pooling (256)
Dense 1 Dense 128 units, ReLU (128)
Dropout Dropout Rate = 0.3 (128)
Output Dense 15 classes, Softmax (15)

What does each term mean?

Conv1D

Convolution Layer — Slides a small "window" across the ECG signal to detect specific patterns like peaks, dips, and rhythms. Think of it as a magnifying glass scanning for clues.

BatchNorm

Batch Normalization — Keeps the data flowing through the network at a consistent scale, preventing the AI from getting "confused" by wildly varying signal amplitudes.

MaxPool

Max Pooling — Compresses the signal by keeping only the most important features from each section. Like summarizing a paragraph into its key sentence.

GAP

Global Average Pooling — Takes the average of all remaining features to create one compact summary before the final decision step.

Dense

Fully Connected Layer — Combines all the detected patterns to reach a final conclusion, like a doctor weighing all the evidence before making a diagnosis.

Dropout

Dropout — Randomly disables 30% of neurons during training so the model doesn't "memorize" — it forces the AI to learn robust, generalizable patterns.

Reading the table columns

  • Configuration — Settings like the number of filters (pattern detectors) and kernel size (how wide the scanning window is).
  • Output Shape — The size of data after the layer processes it; the numbers shrink as the signal is distilled into key insights.
  • ReLU / Softmax — Activation functions; ReLU means "ignore negatives" and Softmax converts final scores into probabilities that add up to 100%.

Understanding the Confidence Score

Every analysis comes with a confidence score — a simple percentage that tells you how certain the AI is about its diagnosis.

What the Score Means

The score ranges from 0% to 100%:

  • 90–100% High confidence. Strong morphological match.
  • 80–89% Moderate. Likely correct, but some noise or ambiguity.
  • Below 80% Low confidence. Manual expert review recommended.

Your Data Stays With You

100% Browser-Based. Zero Data Sent.

Unlike cloud-based AI tools, CardioAI runs the entire analysis directly in your browser using TensorFlow.js. Your ECG data never leaves your device — not even for a millisecond.

How local processing works — step by step

1

You open CardioAI in your browser

The first time you visit, your browser downloads the AI model file (~4 MB) — roughly the size of a single photo. This model contains everything the AI needs to analyze ECGs. It gets saved in your browser's cache, so it only downloads once.

2

You upload an ECG image

When you select an ECG file, it stays 100% on your device. The file is read directly by your browser — it is never uploaded to any server, cloud storage, or external service. The image goes from your file system straight into your browser's memory.

3

The AI analyzes it — right here, right now

Using a technology called TensorFlow.js, the AI model runs entirely inside your browser tab, using your device's own processor (CPU/GPU). The ECG signal is processed through the neural network layers, pattern matching and classification happen in real time — all without any network requests.

4

Results appear instantly — nothing is stored

The analysis results (detected conditions, confidence scores, risk assessment) are shown on screen. When you close the tab or navigate away, everything is gone — no logs, no traces, no saved copies. There is no database, no user account, and no server keeping records.

Works Without Internet

Once the model is cached in your browser, you can disconnect from the internet entirely and CardioAI will still work. This makes it ideal for:

  • 🏥 Clinics with unreliable or restricted internet access
  • ✈️ Remote or field-based medical setups
  • 🔒 High-security environments where network access is limited
  • 📱 Mobile devices on the go with spotty connectivity
The model file is stored in your browser's standard cache. You can clear it anytime through your browser settings — giving you full control.

Why This Matters for Patient Privacy

Medical data is among the most sensitive personal information. Traditional AI tools require uploading patient data to external servers, creating risks of:

  • ⚠️ Data breaches and unauthorized access
  • ⚠️ Third-party data retention and profiling
  • ⚠️ Compliance violations (HIPAA, GDPR)
  • ⚠️ Patient trust erosion
CardioAI eliminates all of these risks by design. Since data never leaves the device, there is nothing to breach, nothing to comply with externally, and nothing to worry about.

Getting Your ECG File

How Do I Transfer My ECG to CardioAI?

ECG machines don't connect to CardioAI via WiFi or Bluetooth. Instead, you export the file from the machine and upload it through your browser — just like attaching a file to an email.

📷

Take a Photo

The simplest method — photograph the printed ECG strip with your phone camera and upload the image (JPG/PNG)

🖨️

Scan the ECG Printout

Use a flatbed scanner or a scanning app on your phone to create a high-quality digital copy (PDF or image)

💾

USB / SD Card Export

Many modern ECG machines save files to USB drives or SD cards. Copy the file to your computer and upload

📧

Email or Cloud

Some machines can email the ECG report as a PDF. Download the attachment and upload to CardioAI

What formats does CardioAI accept?

CardioAI accepts standard image formats — JPG, PNG, BMP, and WEBP. If your ECG machine exports in PDF, simply take a screenshot of the relevant page or use a PDF-to-image converter. For best results, ensure the image clearly shows the full 12-lead ECG waveform without obstructions.

Common Questions About CardioAI

Answers to the questions most frequently asked by clinicians, administrators, and investors.

How does a huge AI model fit in just ~4 MB?

Great question — and the answer is well-documented computer science, not magic. Three techniques work together:

  • Quantization — The model's internal numbers are stored at lower precision (8-bit integers instead of 32-bit decimals). This alone cuts the file size by ~75%, with virtually no loss in accuracy. Think of it like rounding ₹99.387 to ₹99 — close enough for all practical purposes.
  • Pruning — The AI removes "lazy" connections (weights close to zero) that contribute almost nothing to the final answer. A well-pruned model can be 90% sparser — meaning 90% of the numbers become zeros.
  • Compression — All those zeros compress extremely well with standard algorithms (like gzip). A 90% sparse model can shrink by 6–10× during download.

Combined, these techniques can reduce a 60 MB research model to ~4 MB for deployment — while retaining 94%+ classification accuracy. This is the same approach used by Google, Apple, and Tesla to run AI models on phones and cars.

Source: TensorFlow Model Optimization Toolkit — tensorflow.org/model_optimization
How do I transfer an ECG from the machine to this app? Is it WiFi or Bluetooth?

Neither. CardioAI is a web application, not a hardware accessory — it doesn't connect directly to ECG machines. Instead, the workflow is:

  • 📷 Photograph the printed ECG strip with your phone
  • 💾 Export the file from the machine via USB/SD card
  • 📧 Email the ECG report as PDF and download it
  • 🖨️ Scan the paper printout using any scanner app

Then simply open CardioAI in your browser and upload the image. It's as easy as attaching a photo to a WhatsApp message. No special cables, no pairing, no app installation required.

Does CardioAI replace a cardiologist?

Absolutely not. CardioAI is a screening and decision-support tool, not a diagnostic replacement. Think of it like spell-check for doctors — it highlights potential issues, but the doctor always makes the final call.

The tool is especially useful for:

  • Primary care doctors who see ECGs but aren't cardiologists — it gives them a second opinion in seconds
  • Rural clinics where a cardiologist may be hours away — it helps prioritize urgent cases for referral
  • Medical students learning to read ECGs — it provides instant educational feedback

Every report includes a clear disclaimer stating that results must be verified by a qualified physician.

Is CardioAI approved by FDA, CDSCO, or any regulatory body?

CardioAI is currently a research and educational demonstration tool. It has not received FDA (USA), CDSCO (India), or CE (Europe) clearance for clinical diagnostic use.

That said, the underlying technology — AI-powered ECG interpretation — is an active area of regulatory approval globally. Companies like AliveCor and Tricog have received CDSCO approval for their AI ECG devices in India (as of 2025).

Our roadmap includes pursuing regulatory clearance as the tool matures and undergoes the required clinical validation trials.

CardioAI should not be used as the sole basis for clinical decisions. Always consult a qualified physician.
Can I use CardioAI on my phone or tablet?

Yes! CardioAI runs in any modern web browser — Chrome, Safari, Firefox, or Edge — on any device. No app download needed.

On a phone, you can directly photograph a paper ECG and upload it from your camera roll. On a tablet or laptop, you can also upload files from USB or email. The AI model runs locally regardless of device type, though analysis may be slightly faster on devices with more processing power.

What happens if the AI gives a wrong result?

No AI system is 100% accurate — and CardioAI is transparent about this. Every result comes with a confidence score that tells you how certain the AI is about its prediction.

  • High confidence (≥85%) — The AI is fairly certain. Still verify with clinical judgment.
  • Moderate confidence (60–85%) — The AI detected something but isn't sure. Prioritize manual review.
  • Low confidence (<60%) — The AI is uncertain. This could be due to image quality, noise, or rare conditions. A specialist review is strongly recommended.

This is by design — we'd rather flag uncertainty than give false assurance. The model achieves a 94.3% F1 score on benchmark datasets, but real-world accuracy depends heavily on image quality.
